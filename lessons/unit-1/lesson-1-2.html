Lesson 1.2: The Epistemic Symmetry Principle

Unit 1: The Big Picture ‚Ä¢ 50-60 minute lesson
üí° "Would you accept a courtroom where only one side has to prove their case?"

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Define the Epistemic Symmetry Principle (ETS) and explain why it matters
  • Identify double standards in evaluating religious vs naturalistic claims
  • Apply ETS to compare resurrection with abiogenesis claims
  • Recognize hidden bias in everyday reasoning about evidence

Vocabulary

Epistemic: Relating to knowledge and how we know things
Symmetry: Balanced treatment; applying the same standards to all claims
Double Standard: Applying different criteria to similar situations unfairly
Bias: Prejudice for or against something based on preconceptions
Naturalism: The philosophical belief that only natural causes exist (no supernatural)

The Golden Rule of Evidence

Imagine This: Your friend gets accused of cheating on a test. The teacher says: "Prove you didn't cheat!" Your friend says: "Well, prove I DID cheat!"

Who has the burden of proof? Most people would say the accuser needs to provide evidence.

But here's where it gets interesting: When it comes to big questions about origins, people flip this rule around depending on what they already believe.

The Epistemic Symmetry Principle (ETS) is simple but powerful:

If we demand proof for Claim A, we must demand equal proof for Claim B

Why This Matters

Most people use different standards for different claims without realizing it. They demand tons of evidence for supernatural claims but accept naturalistic claims with minimal evidence.

This isn't about being mean or "gotcha" questioning - it's about intellectual honesty. If we're going to seek truth, we need to apply consistent standards.

Example: The Double Standard

Skeptic: "Prove God exists! I need evidence!"

Fair Response: "Okay, but first prove life started by accident. What's your evidence for abiogenesis?"

Skeptic: "Well, we're here, so it must have happened naturally."

But that's NOT evidence ‚Äî that's just assuming the answer!

Notice what happened:

  • Resurrection: "Show me the mechanism, the witnesses, the physical evidence!"
  • Abiogenesis: "It must have happened somehow" (no mechanism required)

This is textbook asymmetry.

Worked Example: Applying ETS to Origins

Let's compare two claims about one-time singular events:

CLAIM A

God created life (theistic explanation)

CLAIM B

Life arose from non-living chemicals by accident (naturalistic explanation)

Asymmetric Approach (Common):

  • Claim A: "Prove it! Show me God in a lab! Where's your mechanism?"
  • Claim B: "We're here, so it must have happened. Science will figure it out eventually."

Symmetric Approach (ETS):

  • Claim A: What evidence exists? (examine fairly)
  • Claim B: What evidence exists? (examine fairly)
  • Compare: Which has better evidential support?

Result when applying ETS:

  • Both are one-time singular events (can't be repeated in lab)
  • Both require inference from present evidence to past event
  • Both should be evaluated using the same historical/evidential standards
  • Neither gets a "free pass" as the "default" position

Hidden Bias in Action

Sports Analogy: Imagine a basketball game where the ref calls every tiny foul on the visiting team but ignores obvious fouls by the home team. Both teams are playing the same game, but the standards are totally different.

That's exactly what happens in debates about evidence. Watch for it:

Watch how different standards get applied in common arguments:

Scenario 1: Ancient Texts

Resurrection: "You can't trust ancient religious texts - they're biased!"

Caesar's Gallic Wars: "Ancient historical texts are generally reliable."

‚Üí Same type of source, different standards applied

Scenario 2: Laws of Nature

Resurrection: "That violates natural law, so it's impossible!"

Big Bang: "Something came from nothing 13.8 billion years ago."

‚Üí Both invoke events outside normal natural processes, different standards applied

Scenario 3: Eyewitness Testimony

Miracle claims: "Eyewitnesses are unreliable - people see what they want to see."

Criminal trials: "We trust eyewitness testimony to convict people."

‚Üí Same type of evidence, different standards applied

The Pattern:

  • Claims we don't like ‚Üí High evidence bar
  • Claims we do like ‚Üí Low or no evidence bar

ETS says: No free passes. Same standards for both.

Where Does Asymmetry Come From?

  1. Cultural Conditioning
    We're taught: "Science = facts, Religion = faith"
    This creates bias before we examine actual evidence
  2. Philosophical Assumptions
    "Naturalism is default, supernaturalism needs extraordinary proof"
    But why? Both are philosophical positions that need justification
  3. Emotional Investment
    We want certain things to be true or false
    This affects how much evidence we demand
  4. Social Pressure
    "Smart people believe X" creates peer pressure
    We don't want to look foolish by questioning accepted narratives

ETS cuts through all of this by asking one simple question: "Am I applying the same standard to both claims?"

Deep Dive: ETS in Historical Method

Think Like a Detective: When detectives investigate a crime, they use the same methods regardless of who the suspect is. Rich or poor, famous or unknown - same evidence standards.

Historians should work the same way. Let's see if they actually do...

How Historians Evaluate Ancient Claims

Standard Historical Criteria:

  1. Multiple independent sources?
  2. Early attestation (how soon after the event)?
  3. Enemy attestation (do hostile sources confirm it)?
  4. Embarrassing details (would you make this up)?
  5. Explanatory power (does it explain the data well)?

These criteria should apply EQUALLY to religious and secular historical claims.

Let's Apply This Symmetrically

Julius Caesar Crossed the Rubicon (49 BC)

  1. Multiple sources: ~5 ancient historians
  2. Early attestation: ~100 years after event
  3. Enemy attestation: Limited
  4. Embarrassing details: Few
  5. Explanatory power: Explains civil war

Jesus' Resurrection (33 AD)

  1. Multiple sources: Paul's creed, 4 Gospels, Acts = ~9 sources
  2. Early attestation: 3-5 years (creed), 20-30 years (Paul's letters)
  3. Enemy attestation: Yes (Jewish leaders never denied empty tomb)
  4. Embarrassing details: Many (women witnesses, disciples fled)
  5. Explanatory power: Explains origin of Christianity, disciple transformation

Observation: When we apply the SAME historical criteria to both events, resurrection evidence is actually stronger than Caesar's Rubicon crossing.

Yet we teach Caesar as fact and resurrection as myth. Why?

Answer: Asymmetric standards based on philosophical assumptions, not evidence.

The Balance Scale Concept

Remember Playground Seesaws? If one kid weighs way more than the other, the seesaw stays tilted. It only works fairly when both sides are balanced.

Evidence demands work the same way. Let's see if debates are actually balanced or secretly tilted:

Think of evidence demands like a balance scale. ETS keeps it fair:

LEFT SIDE

"Resurrection needs proof!"

Demand: High evidence bar

RIGHT SIDE

"Naturalistic alternatives need proof too!"

Demand: Same high evidence bar

ETS keeps the scale balanced. Both sides get the same treatment.

How the Scale Gets Tipped (Unfairly)

Pattern #1: "Default Position" Bias

  • "Naturalism is the default, supernatural needs to overcome that"
  • This tips the scale BEFORE looking at evidence
  • Fair approach: Both positions start neutral, evidence decides

Pattern #2: "Extraordinary Claims" Bias

  • "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence"
  • Sounds fair, but who decides what's "extraordinary"?
  • Is abiogenesis (life from non-life) extraordinary? Most say no.
  • Is resurrection (life from death) extraordinary? Most say yes.
  • But BOTH are one-time unrepeatable singularities!
  • ETS says: Same type of claim ‚Üí same evidence standard

Pattern #3: "Science vs Religion" Bias

  • "Science is evidence-based, religion is faith-based"
  • This assumes the answer before examining the evidence
  • Christianity CLAIMS to be evidence-based (resurrection witnesses)
  • Fair approach: Evaluate that claim fairly, don't dismiss it categorically

Practice Scenarios: Applying ETS Step-by-Step

Time to Level Up: Reading about ETS is like watching someone play a video game. Now it's your turn to actually play.

Here are three common arguments you'll hear. We'll break down exactly how to spot the hidden asymmetry using our 4-step process.

Scenario 1: The Ancient Text Challenge

Skeptic: "The Bible was written by biased believers. We can't trust it."

Christian: "But we trust other ancient texts written by participants in events."

Step 1: Identify the Standard Being Applied

‚Üí Ancient texts from biased sources are unreliable

Step 2: Apply Same Standard to Similar Claims

  • Caesar wrote about his own conquests (biased participant)
  • Josephus wrote Jewish history (biased participant)
  • Thucydides wrote Peloponnesian War history (biased participant)

Step 3: Check for Consistency

‚Üí Do we reject ALL ancient texts from participants?

‚Üí Or do we evaluate them using historical methods regardless of source?

Step 4: ETS Conclusion

  • Apply standard historical methods to ALL ancient texts
  • Bias doesn't automatically disqualify - evaluate credibility case by case
  • Gospel writers: Multiple independent sources, early attestation, embarrassing details
  • When evaluated symmetrically, Gospel evidence is strong

Scenario 2: The "Extraordinary Evidence" Challenge

Skeptic: "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. Resurrection is extraordinary, so you need overwhelming proof."

Christian: "Is abiogenesis extraordinary? What about the Big Bang?"

Step 1: Define "Extraordinary"

What makes a claim extraordinary?

  • One-time singular event?
  • Unrepeatable?
  • Outside normal experience?

Step 2: Apply Definition Consistently

  • Resurrection: One-time singular event ‚úì
  • Abiogenesis: One-time singular event ‚úì
  • Big Bang: One-time singular event ‚úì

Step 3: Check Evidence Standards

‚Üí Are we demanding "extraordinary evidence" for ALL three?

‚Üí Or just for the one we don't like?

Step 4: ETS Conclusion

  • If resurrection needs extraordinary evidence (because it's a singular event), then so do abiogenesis and Big Bang
  • If we accept abiogenesis/Big Bang on inference from present evidence, we should evaluate resurrection the same way
  • Result: Resurrection has direct eyewitness testimony; abiogenesis has none

Scenario 3: The "Miracles Are Impossible" Challenge

Skeptic: "Miracles violate the laws of nature. Natural laws are never violated. Therefore miracles are impossible."

Christian: "Did the Big Bang violate the law of conservation of energy?"

Step 1: Identify the Standard

‚Üí Events that violate natural laws are impossible

Step 2: Apply to All Claims

  • Does the Big Bang violate conservation laws? (Something from nothing)
  • Does abiogenesis violate probability laws? (Life from non-life)
  • Does consciousness violate materialist assumptions? (Mind from matter)

Step 3: Check for Special Pleading

‚Üí Is the skeptic allowing naturalistic exceptions but not theistic ones?

"Natural laws are ALWAYS true... except when they're not... but only for natural explanations"

Step 4: ETS Conclusion

  • Either natural laws can be transcended/initiated, or they can't
  • If Big Bang can transcend conservation laws, resurrection can transcend death
  • The question isn't "Can laws be transcended?" but "What's the best explanation for the evidence?"
  • Both should be evaluated on evidential grounds, not a priori ruled out

Discussion Questions

  1. Can you think of a time when you applied different standards to different claims? What was the situation?
  2. Why do you think people are more skeptical of supernatural claims than naturalistic ones?
  3. Is it fair to say "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence"? What makes a claim "extraordinary"?
  4. If someone demands proof that God exists, what proof should they provide that He doesn't?
  5. How would debates about religion change if everyone used ETS?
