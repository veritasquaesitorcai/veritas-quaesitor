Lesson 1.2: The Epistemic Symmetry Principle

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Define the Epistemic Symmetry Principle (ETS) and explain why it matters
  • Identify double standards in evaluating religious vs naturalistic claims
  • Apply ETS to compare resurrection with abiogenesis claims
  • Recognize hidden bias in everyday reasoning about evidence

Vocabulary

Epistemic: Relating to knowledge and how we know things
Symmetry: Balanced treatment; applying the same standards to all claims
Double Standard: Applying different criteria to similar situations unfairly
Bias: Prejudice for or against something based on preconceptions
Naturalism: The philosophical belief that only natural causes exist (no supernatural)

The Golden Rule of Evidence

The Epistemic Symmetry Principle (ETS) is simple but powerful:

If we demand proof for Claim A, we must demand equal proof for Claim B

Why This Matters

Most people use different standards for different claims without realizing it. They demand tons of evidence for supernatural claims but accept naturalistic claims with minimal evidence.

Example: The Double Standard

Skeptic: "Prove God exists! I need evidence!"

Fair Response: "Okay, but first prove life started by accident. What's your evidence for abiogenesis?"

Often the skeptic will say: "Well, we're here, so it must have happened naturally." But that's NOT evidence - that's just assuming the answer!

Hidden Bias in Action

Watch how different standards get applied:

  • Resurrection: "That's extraordinary! You need massive evidence!"
  • Abiogenesis: "Life must have started somehow. It's just science."

Notice the difference? One gets challenged, the other gets a free pass. ETS says: No free passes. Same standards for both.

The Balance Scale Concept

Think of evidence demands like a balance scale. ETS keeps it fair:

LEFT SIDE

"Resurrection needs proof!"

Demand: High evidence bar

Discussion Questions

  1. Can you think of a time when you applied different standards to different claims? What was the situation?
  2. Why do you think people are more skeptical of supernatural claims than naturalistic ones?
  3. Is it fair to say "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence"? What makes a claim "extraordinary"?
  4. If someone demands proof that God exists, what proof should they provide that He doesn't?
  5. How would debates about religion change if everyone used ETS?

Activity: "Spot the Asymmetry"

Instructions: Read each pair of statements. Identify if there's an asymmetric standard being applied.

Pair 1:

Statement A: "You can't trust ancient religious texts - they're biased."

Statement B: "Ancient historical texts about Caesar are reliable."

Question: Same standard applied? _______________

Pair 2:

Statement A: "The resurrection violates natural law, so it's impossible."

Statement B: "The Big Bang created something from nothing."

Question: Same standard applied? _______________

Pair 3:

Statement A: "Eyewitness testimony about miracles is unreliable."

Statement B: "We trust eyewitness testimony in criminal trials."

Question: Same standard applied? _______________

Homework

  1. Watch: Find a debate about religion online (YouTube, podcast, etc.)
    Task: Count how many times you see asymmetric standards being applied. Write down 3 examples.
  2. Journal Prompt: "Have I ever demanded evidence for something I disagreed with but accepted something I agreed with without much evidence? What was it?"
  3. Challenge: Practice using ETS in a conversation this week. When someone makes a claim, ask: "What evidence supports that?" - then apply the same standard to competing claims.
