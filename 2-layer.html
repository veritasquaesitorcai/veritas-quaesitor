The Resurrection Question

A Two-Layer Protocol for Evidence-Based Examination

The Protocol

Analyze the resurrection of Jesus in two distinct layers: (1) a historical IBE evaluation of the resurrection claim using early sources (esp. 1 Cor 15) without ruling out miracles a priori, and (2) a separate discussion of what Jesus' identity would imply if the resurrection occurred. Do not collapse historical analysis into theological belief.

Why This Works

  • Separates evidence examination (Layer 1) from identity conclusion (Layer 2)
  • Removes the category error: resurrection isn't the belief‚Äîit's the evidence
  • Christianity itself invites investigation (1 Cor 15:6: witnesses still living)
  • Paul didn't argue FOR resurrection‚Äîhe argued FROM it to Jesus' identity

Understanding the Layers

Layer 1: Historical Evidence Examination

This is standard historical analysis using Inference to Best Explanation (IBE). It evaluates testimony, temporal proximity, enemy attestation, and alternative hypotheses‚Äîthe same methodology applied to any historical event. No faith commitment is required to examine whether the evidence supports the claim that witnesses reported encountering a risen Jesus.

Layer 2: Identity Implications

Given the historical evidence from Layer 1, what would it mean for Jesus' identity claims if the resurrection occurred? This is philosophical/theological reasoning informed by evidence. This is where personal conclusions are drawn, but only after honest examination of the historical foundation.

The Critical Distinction

Paul himself operated this way in 1 Corinthians 15. He didn't argue for the resurrection‚Äîhe listed witnesses and said "most are still living" (go check!). His argument was from the resurrection to its implications. He said if it didn't happen, Christians would be "most to be pitied" as false witnesses. The evidence layer was treated as investigable fact; the identity layer was the conclusion drawn from it.
